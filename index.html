🦠

What's happening

In SIBO, too many bacteria live in your small intestine. When they reach fermentable carbs, they ferment them into gas — driving bloating, pain, and irregular bowels.

🔤

Meet FODMAPs

The main culprits are FODMAPs — Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides And Polyols. Think wheat, onion, garlic, beans, milk sugar, and certain fruits.

✅

The safe core

Foods that don't feed bacteria are your foundation: meat, fish, eggs, most non-starchy veg, low-sugar fruit, rice, and hard cheese. Protein and fat are essentially "free."

⏱️

Mind the gap

Leave 4–5 hours between meals where you can. The gap lets your gut's "cleaning wave" sweep bacteria onward. Constant snacking keeps switching it off.

Which "SIBO diet" is right?

There isn't one official diet. These are the main expert approaches — they overlap a lot. Work with a clinician to choose.

How long do I have to eat like this?

The strict phase is usually short — about 2–6 weeks — to calm symptoms. It's not meant to be permanent. After treatment you methodically reintroduce foods to find your personal limits, and many foods are tolerated far better than before.

Why are onion and garlic singled out so often?

They're exceptionally high in fructans and turn up in almost every savoury dish, sauce and stock. The fix is easy: garlic- and onion-infused oil gives you the flavour because the FODMAPs don't dissolve into oil. Note the low-fermentation (Cedars-Sinai) approach actually permits them — so some people tolerate them fine.

Is this the same as gluten-free?

Not quite. Wheat is limited because of its fructans, not gluten itself. That's why genuine sourdough and small portions are sometimes tolerated, and why naturally gluten-free grains like rice and quinoa are perfect.

Do I have to give up all my favourite foods forever?

No — and that's the message of this whole site. Portion size matters enormously, swaps cover most cravings, and reintroduction widens your plate again. Over-restricting long-term can actually starve your beneficial bacteria.
